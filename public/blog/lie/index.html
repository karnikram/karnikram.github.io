Lie Groups and Lie Algebra

September 3, 2017

Motivation

In robot vision we frequently deal with problems where the motion of an object is to be estimated. For instance, if a drone is being moved around in a scene and we were to reconstruct a 3D model of the scene using the images from the drone, we need a way to model and represent the trajectory of the camera to know where the images were taken from. The motion of the drone is typically modeled as a rigid body motion (a rotation and a translation, more formally called a Special Euclidean Transformation \(SE(3)\)), a topic which has been studied extensively.

A rigid body motion can be represented in multiple ways and our choice of representation is closely tied to the application. For instance, the 3D + YPR representation is a popular choice within the space of aerial vehicles but not so much outside of it because of its famous gimbal lock problem. Consider the homogeneous matrix representation of a transformation \(\mathrm{T}\),

\[ T = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} & & & x\\ & R & & y\\ & & & z\\ \hline 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] \]

where \(R\) is a 3 x 3 rotation matrix (more formally called a Special Orthogonal Transformation \(SO(3)\))

We will now derive the exponential coordinates representation of an infinitesimal rotation.

Consider a rotational trajectory \(R(t) : \mathbb{R} \rightarrow SO(3)\), the rotations must satisfy the following by definition.

$$ R(t)R(t)^{\top} = I$$ Computing the derivative of the above equation with respect to time \(t\), we obtain $$ \dot{R}(t)R^\top(t) + R(t)\smash{\dot{R}}^\top(t) = 0 $$ $$ \Rightarrow\dot{R}(t)R^\top(t) = -(\dot{R}(t)R^\top(t))^\top $$ The resulting equation shows that \(\dot{R}(t)R^\top(t)\) is a skew-symmetric matrix, which implies that there must exist a vector \(w(t)\in \mathbb{R}^3\) such that $$ \dot{R}(t)R^\top(t) = \hat{\omega}(t) \Leftrightarrow\dot{R}(t) = \omega(t)R(t) $$ Now if \(R(t_0) = I\), we have \(\dot{R}(t_0) = \hat{\omega}(t_0)\) and so a Taylor series approximation of \(R(t)\) about \(t = t_0\) would result in $$ R(t_o + dt) \approx I_o + \hat{\omega}(t_o)dt $$

What this means is that infinitesimal rotations around the identity are locally only dependent on the three dimensional \((\omega_1,\omega_2,\omega_3)\) space of skew symmetric matrices, also called the tangent space. This tangent space at the identity is more formally defined as the lie algebra \(so(3)\) of the lie group \(SO(3)\).

$$ so(3) \stackrel{.}{=} \{ \hat{\omega} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 x 3} \;|\; \omega \in \mathbb{R}^{3}\} $$

This tangent space is a linear space with three degrees of freedom and no additional constraints which makes it much easier to work with than its corresponding lie group for motion estimation and optimization problems.

We shall now try to derive the mapping that will allow us to represent elements of the lie group \(SO(3)\) in terms of its lie algebra \(so(3)\).

From the above discussion we know,

\[ \dot{R}(t) = \hat{\omega}R(t)\,,\,R(t_o) = I \]

which can be interpreted as a linear ordinary equation with inital state at \(I\), and hence its solution is given by

\[ \boxed{R(t) = e^{\hat{\omega}t}} \]

where \(e^{\hat{\omega}t}\) is the matrix exponential

\[ e^{\hat{\omega}t} = I + \hat{\omega}t + \frac{(\hat{\omega}t)^2}{2!} + \frac{(\hat{\omega}t)^3}{3!} + …
\]

A useful interpretation of this solution is that of a rotation around the axis \(\omega \in \mathbb{R}^3\) by an angle of \(t\) radians.

Thus we define an exponential map \[ exp: so(3) \rightarrow SO(3); \; \hat{\omega} \mapsto e^{\hat{\omega}} \]

\[ R = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} r_{11} & r_{12} & r_{13} \\ r_{21} & r_{21} & r_{23} \\ r_{31} & r_{32} & r_{33} \\ \end{array} \right] \]
\[ \boxed{\Vert{\omega}\Vert = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{trace(R) - 1}{2}\right), \; \frac{\omega}{\Vert{\omega}\Vert} = \frac{1}{2\sin{(\Vert{\omega}\Vert)}} \left[ \begin{array}{c} r_{32} - r_{23}\\ r_{13} - r_{31}\\ r_{21} - r_{12} \end{array} \right]} \]
\[ \boxed{ e^{\hat{\omega}} = I + \frac{\hat{\omega}}{\Vert{\omega}\Vert}\sin{\Vert{\omega}\Vert} + \frac{\hat{\omega}^2}{\Vert{\omega}\Vert^2}(1 - \cos(\Vert\omega\Vert)) } \]
